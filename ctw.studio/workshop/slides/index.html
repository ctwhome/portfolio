BLOCK 1

Context

How AI actually works

AI is a prediction machine

At its core, a large language model (LLM) predicts the next word.

You type text → the model predicts the most likely continuation.

"Today is a lovely day. I would like to go to the..."

park? beach? library? The model picks statistically.

Two phases

Training

Reading every book in the library

  • Costs millions of dollars
  • Takes months
  • Done by companies (OpenAI, Anthropic, Google)
  • Happens once (then updated)

Inference

Answering a question from memory

  • Fast (seconds)
  • Cheap per query
  • What happens when you type a prompt
  • Happens every time you use AI

Tokens: how AI reads

AI doesn't read words — it reads tokens.

'university' = 1 token

'epistemology' = 3 tokens

'AI Literacy Workshop' = 3 tokens

Tokens are how models process text and how you're billed.

Try it: tokenizer.dev

Context window: AI's working memory

How much text the model can "see" at once.

  • GPT-4: ~128K tokens (~300 pages)
  • Claude: ~200K tokens (~500 pages)
  • Gemini: ~1M tokens (~2,500 pages)

This is why you can upload entire papers and ask questions about them.

VOCABULARY

Foundation Layer

  • Frontier model — The most advanced AI available today (GPT-4.5, Claude Opus, Gemini Ultra)
  • LLM — Large Language Model. The engine behind ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini
  • Multimodal — Handles text + images + audio + video. Upload a handwritten exam → transcribed and analyzed

Accuracy Layer

  • Hallucination — When AI confidently makes things up. Citing papers that don't exist.
  • Grounding — Giving AI trusted sources so it stays factual. "Only answer based on these PDFs."
  • RAG — Retrieval-Augmented Generation. The model searches a knowledge base before answering.
  • Temperature — Controls randomness. Low = predictable (grading). High = creative (brainstorming).

Action Layer

  • Tools — External capabilities AI can call: search, calculate, browse, send email
  • Orchestration — The conductor coordinating model + tools + data behind the scenes
  • Agents — AI that plans and executes multi-step tasks autonomously
  • Autonomy — How independently the AI operates (spectrum: scripted → semi-autonomous → fully autonomous)

Development Layer

  • Fine-tuning — Retraining a model on specialized data (expensive, rare, last resort)
  • Context engineering — Designing the instructions and information you give AI (this is the real skill)
  • Prompt — The instruction you give AI. The question, task, or request.

Who builds what

  • OpenAI (GPT-4.5, GPT-5) — first mover, ChatGPT, strong general tasks
  • Anthropic (Claude) — safety-focused, exceptional with long documents, Projects
  • Google (Gemini) — Google Workspace integration, strong multimodal
  • Meta (Llama) — open-source, free, can run locally
  • Mistral — European, open-source, strong multilingual

Open-source vs closed

Closed / Commercial

  • ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini
  • Best performance
  • Easy to use
  • Data goes to their servers
  • Subscription costs

Open-source

  • Llama, Mistral, DeepSeek
  • Free to use
  • Can run on your laptop
  • Data stays private
  • Requires some setup

The gap is narrowing fast. Today's frontier is next year's baseline.

What you now know

  • AI predicts the next word — it's not "thinking"
  • Training vs inference: building the brain vs using it
  • Tokens, context windows, temperature — the dials you can turn
  • The vocabulary to talk about AI without guessing
  • Who builds what and why it matters

Tools

What's available today

The major players

A quick tour — what each does best.

ChatGPT

OpenAI

  • The most popular AI tool in the world
  • Free tier (limited) vs Plus ($20/mo) vs Pro ($200/mo)
  • GPTs: custom assistants for specific tasks
  • Best for: general tasks, image generation (DALL-E), voice conversations
  • Limitation: shorter context window than Claude

Gemini

Google

  • Deep integration with Google Workspace (Docs, Sheets, Gmail)
  • Strong multimodal: images, video, YouTube analysis
  • Best for: people already in the Google ecosystem
  • NotebookLM: upload sources, get a podcast-style summary

Local & Open-Source LLMs

  • Ollama, LM Studio — run models on your own laptop
  • Complete data privacy — nothing leaves your machine
  • Free, no subscription
  • Best for: sensitive data, offline use, experimentation
  • Trade-off: less capable than frontier models (for now)

Why we're focusing on Claude

  • 200K token context — upload 500 pages and ask questions
  • Projects: persistent knowledge base + custom instructions
  • Chrome extension for research on any web page
  • Excellent academic writing quality
  • Honest about uncertainty — says "I don't know" when it doesn't

BLOCK 3

Future

What's coming and what it means

The next 12-24 months

  • Agents — AI that doesn't just answer, but plans and executes multi-step tasks
  • Computer use — AI that can see and interact with your screen
  • Research workflows — automated literature monitoring, cross-language sources, dataset analysis
  • Personalized education — AI tutors adapted to individual students

AI as research amplifier

The tedious parts get automated.

The thinking that requires human judgment stays human.

  • Interpretation
  • Ethics
  • Creativity
  • Mentorship
"The academics who engage with AI will shape how it's used. The ones who don't will have it shaped for them."

Benefits for academics

  • Democratizes access to research tools (smaller institutions, limited funding)
  • Reduces administrative burden (grading, emails, reports)
  • Enables new research methods (large-scale text analysis, cross-language research)
  • Levels the playing field for non-native English speakers

Risks to discuss honestly

  • Plagiarism & integrity — detection is unreliable. Policy matters more than policing.
  • Institutional policies — most universities are still figuring this out
  • Citation of AI content — emerging standards (APA 7th ed. has guidelines)
  • Data privacy — what happens when you upload student work to cloud AI?
  • Bias — LLMs inherit biases from training data. Critical for social sciences.
  • Over-reliance — skill atrophy when you can't write without AI

These risks are manageable. Not engaging is the bigger risk.
